Understanding Dog Bite Laws in Texas

If you’re a dog owner in Texas, it’s important to understand the laws surrounding dog bites to ensure that you are complying with legal requirements and that you and your dog are protected in the event of an incident. In Houston, TX, dog owners must adhere to state and local laws regarding dog bites.

Under Texas law, dog owners are responsible for any damages or injuries caused by their dogs. This means that if your dog bites someone, you may be held liable for any resulting medical expenses, lost wages, or other damages. However, there are some exceptions to this rule, such as if the person who was bitten provoked the dog or was trespassing on your property.

In Houston, there are also additional laws regarding dog bites. For example, the city requires that all dogs over four months old be licensed and vaccinated against rabies. If your dog bites someone and it is determined that the dog was not properly vaccinated against rabies, you may face additional fines and penalties. It’s important to take these laws seriously and ensure that your dog is up-to-date on all vaccinations and licenses to avoid any legal issues down the line.


Texas Dog Bite Law


Under Texas law, a dog owner is strictly liable for any injuries caused by their pet if the dog had a previous history of aggressive behavior or if the owner was aware of the dog’s aggressive tendencies. This means that the owner can be held responsible for damages even if they did not know that their dog was capable of biting someone.

Additionally, Texas law allows for the “negligence” theory of liability. This means that a dog owner can be held responsible for injuries caused by their pet if they were negligent in controlling or restraining the dog. For example, if a dog owner fails to leash their dog in a public area and the dog bites someone, the owner may be held liable for any resulting injuries.

The common causes of dog bites in Texas

  • Lack of training or socialization: Dogs that have not been properly trained or socialized may exhibit aggressive behavior towards humans, including biting.
  • Protective or territorial behavior: Dogs may bite when they perceive a threat to their territory or family, such as when a stranger enters their home or yard.
  • Fear or anxiety: Dogs that are fearful or anxious may bite when they feel threatened or cornered, such as in crowded public spaces or when approached by unfamiliar people or animals.
  • Provocation: Dogs may bite if they feel threatened, teased, or provoked, such as when children play too roughly with them or when people pull their tails or ears.
  • Medical issues: Dogs with medical conditions or injuries may bite if they are in pain or feel vulnerable, such as when their ears or paws are touched.
